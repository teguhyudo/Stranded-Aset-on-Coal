\section{Policy Implications}
\label{sec:discussion}


Our findings carry significant implications for climate-financial policy in Indonesia and, by extension, for other coal-dependent emerging economies.

\subsection{Financial Regulation and Supervision}

The stress test results reveal that coal transition risk is a near-term supervisory challenge. While no bank breaches regulatory capital thresholds, the most exposed D-SIB sees its CAR decline by up to 0.92 percentage points, and total system losses reach 3.62\% of bank equity under the 1.5\textdegree C scenario. These findings support the case for OJK to incorporate climate transition risk into its supervisory framework, including:

\textit{Enhanced disclosure requirements.} Banks should be required to disclose their fossil fuel exposures at the borrower level, disaggregated by sector and climate scenario vulnerability. Our analysis was constrained by the limited granularity of public loan disclosures; standardised reporting templates would enable more precise risk assessment. OJK's circular SEOJK~14/2023 on sustainable finance disclosure represents a first step, but it stops short of requiring the borrower-level coal exposure breakdown needed for network-based stress testing. The NGFS's publication of standardised climate scenario inputs \citep{NGFS2022} provides an international reference point for the kind of disclosure that would be most valuable.

\textit{Climate-adjusted capital requirements.} OJK should consider implementing differentiated risk weights for coal-exposed lending, reflecting the elevated probability of default under climate transition scenarios. Our LGD estimates (ranging from 0.10 for distressed firms to 1.0 for defaulting firms) could inform the calibration of such adjustments. A practical starting point would be a Pillar~2 supervisory add-on for D-SIBs with coal exposure shares exceeding a defined threshold, calibrated to the scenario-conditional expected loss rates from our stress test. A 25~basis point capital add-on for coal-exposed lending, applied to the six banks with identified coal exposures exceeding \$200 million, would require approximately \$1.8 billion in additional capital --- a feasible ask given current CAR levels well above the minimum, but one that would meaningfully reduce the system's vulnerability to a disorderly transition.

\textit{Concentration limits.} The finding that a single bank (BMRI) bears over 53\% of total system coal credit losses underscores the need for sector-specific concentration limits in coal-related lending. Current OJK regulations impose single-borrower exposure limits but do not specifically address sectoral concentration in climate-sensitive sectors. An adaptation of the Large Exposure Framework under Basel III, setting a coal-sector limit of 20\% of Tier~1 capital for D-SIBs and 15\% for smaller banks, would cap the potential loss from a correlated coal default event and align with the network-based systemic risk considerations identified in our analysis.

\textit{CKPN as a regulatory buffer.} Indonesian banks' existing loan loss allowances (CKPN) provide a first line of defence against coal-related credit losses. Our CKPN-adjusted stress test shows that pre-existing provisions can absorb a portion of the estimated losses, reducing the capital impact. However, CKPN buffers are designed for expected losses across the entire loan portfolio and may be insufficient if coal stranding triggers simultaneous losses across multiple borrowers. OJK should consider requiring banks with significant coal exposure to maintain sector-specific provisioning reserves that reflect forward-looking transition risk, consistent with the IFRS~9 expected credit loss framework. Our estimated additional CKPN requirements --- \$220--430 million system-wide depending on the scenario --- would represent a manageable but significant increase in provisioning discipline, analogous to the sector-specific provisioning guidance issued by the ECB for real estate exposure under its climate stress testing programme \citep{ECB2022}.

\textit{Climate scenario disclosures aligned with NGFS frameworks.} Indonesian D-SIBs should be required to conduct annual internal climate stress tests using NGFS Phase IV scenarios and to publish summary results in their Pillar~3 disclosures. This requirement, analogous to the Bank of England's Climate Biennial Exploratory Scenario, would build institutional capacity for scenario analysis within banks and provide OJK with a basis for comparing institutions' self-assessed transition risk against the supervisory benchmark provided by our external stress test methodology.

\subsection{Fiscal Policy}

Coal transition risk carries material fiscal implications. The coal sector contributes approximately \$8.5 billion annually (4.5\% of central government revenue) through royalties, corporate taxes, and SOE dividends \citep{IESR2023}. Under a 2\textdegree C scenario, the burden would fall disproportionately on local government transfers (Dana Bagi Hasil) in coal-producing provinces.

Why do Indonesian banks continue expanding coal lending if transition risk is material? The explanation combines a ``tragedy of the horizon'' --- bank loan tenors (3--7 years) are shorter than transition risk horizons \citep{Carattini2023} --- with implicit sovereign guarantees for state-connected borrowers, persistent information asymmetries (most banks lack climate scenario analysis infrastructure), and regulatory forbearance during the 2021--2023 coal price supercycle that relaxed supervisory pressure precisely when transition risk was accumulating.

The financial system's capacity to absorb transition losses is adequate at the aggregate level but unevenly distributed. An orderly, gradual transition would minimise credit channel amplification; a disorderly transition could trigger bank deleveraging spirals \citep{Bernanke1999, Roncoroni2021}.



\subsection{Implications for Other Coal-Dependent Economies}
\label{subsec:comparative}

The coal-banking-sovereign nexus we document in Indonesia is not unique to this country. Similar structural configurations exist across a range of coal-dependent emerging economies, and our framework provides a replicable template for country-specific assessments.

\textit{South Africa} presents the closest parallel. South Africa's thermal coal sector, while smaller than Indonesia's in absolute output, accounts for a comparable share of GDP and export earnings. The country's major banks --- Standard Bank, FirstRand, Absa, and Nedbank --- have disclosed significant coal sector exposures and face similar tension between managing stranded asset risk and supporting the just-transition financing requirements of JETP South Africa (\$8.5 billion). South Africa's more advanced capital markets and somewhat more diversified economy may provide greater shock absorption capacity than Indonesia, but the sovereign-bank nexus is tighter given Eskom's central role and the government's implicit guarantees.

India, Colombia, and the Philippines face analogous challenges with varying structural features: India's public sector banks carry larger absolute coal exposures on already-stressed balance sheets; Colombia's coal export dependence (approximately 12\% of exports) creates fiscal vulnerability; and the Philippines faces transition risk primarily through coal-fired power utilities rather than mining.

The cross-country comparison underscores two general findings from our Indonesia analysis that are likely to carry over: first, that the distribution of coal credit losses across banks is more important than the aggregate system-level impact for supervisory purposes; and second, that the interaction between fiscal revenue loss and banking sector stress --- the sovereign-bank feedback loop at the heart of the coal-banking-sovereign nexus --- requires integrated assessment rather than sectoral analysis. Our bipartite network methodology, calibrated to Indonesia, provides a blueprint for replicating this integrated assessment in other jurisdictions where the data allow.

Our estimates (\$60.1 billion under 2\textdegree C, \$99.2 billion under 1.5\textdegree C) are broadly consistent with global estimates when scaled to Indonesia's 3\% share of global coal reserves \citep{Mercure2018}. Among Indonesia-specific studies, \citet{sonny2016}'s central estimate of \$86 billion (using a cash cost approach on 16 firms) falls between our 2\textdegree C and 1.5\textdegree C estimates, lending cross-validation despite methodological differences. \citet{Rishanty2023} estimate a larger total VaR of \$206 billion, but this covers the entire coal value chain; their investor component (\$35 billion) is more comparable to our mining-firm-focused estimates. \citet{IESR2023} identify ``significant'' banking risk qualitatively but do not construct the bipartite exposure network that drives our credit loss estimates.

Relative to advanced-economy stress tests, our maximum CAR impact (0.92 percentage points for the most exposed D-SIB) is comparable to the ECB's 2022 climate stress test (0.5--1.5 percentage points) \citep{ECB2022}, though Indonesian D-SIBs' substantial capital buffers (median CAR 23.3\%) mean these losses are absorbed without breaching regulatory thresholds.

\subsubsection{Parameter Sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:tornado} presents the tornado diagram for aggregate stranded value under the 1.5\textdegree C scenario, showing the sensitivity to individual parameter perturbations.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_10.pdf}
\caption{Tornado diagram: sensitivity of aggregate stranded value (1.5\textdegree C scenario) to individual parameter perturbations. Each bar shows the range of aggregate SV when the indicated parameter is varied while holding others at baseline values. The vertical dashed line marks the baseline estimate.}
\label{fig:tornado}
\end{figure}

The discount rate is the most influential parameter: a 2 percentage point increase in the WACC reduces aggregate SV by approximately 15\%, while a 2 percentage point decrease increases SV by approximately 18\%. This asymmetry reflects the convex relationship between discount rates and NPV. The coal price path is the second most important factor, followed by production volume and mine life assumptions. Regarding mine life, our baseline assumes a uniform $T = 20$ years for all firms, reflecting the standard Indonesian coal concession duration. Table~\ref{tab:robustness}, Panel~B reports the sensitivity of aggregate stranded values to mine life assumptions of $T \in \{10, 15, 20, 25\}$ years. Shortening the mine life to $T = 10$ reduces the 1.5\textdegree C aggregate SV from \$92.6 billion to \$60.5 billion (a 35\% reduction), while extending to $T = 25$ increases it to \$99.2 billion (a 7\% increase), reflecting the diminishing marginal contribution of distant cash flows at positive discount rates. The number of defaulting firms also declines with shorter mine lives: under 1.5\textdegree C, defaults fall from 9 at $T = 20$ to 8 at $T = 10$, as some marginal firms remain viable over shorter horizons. This sensitivity underscores the importance of mine-specific concession duration data, which we do not currently have.

Scenario probability weights have a relatively modest effect, as the expected SV is a weighted average that is bounded by the 2\textdegree C and 1.5\textdegree C point estimates.

\subsubsection{Alternative Model Specifications}

We test the sensitivity of bank stress test results to several alternative specifications (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}, Panels~C and~D). Varying the generic LGD from 0.45 (baseline) to 0.55 increases total 1.5\textdegree C bank losses modestly from \$3.2 billion to \$3.3 billion. Excluding indirect mining loan losses entirely reduces losses to \$2.4 billion, confirming that direct bipartite exposures drive the majority of the result. Reducing the indirect NPL shock from 15\% to 7.5\% yields losses of \$2.8 billion. No bank breaches any regulatory capital threshold under any specification, reflecting the substantial capital buffers maintained by Indonesian banks. Our baseline results thus lie in the middle of a plausible range.

\subsubsection{DMO Price Floor Sensitivity}

Our baseline NPV model applies a single export price path to each firm's entire production. In practice, Indonesia's Domestic Market Obligation (DMO) requires producers to sell at least 25\% of output domestically at a capped price of \$70/t. We test the sensitivity of stranded values to incorporating this price floor. Under the DMO specification, aggregate SV under 1.5\textdegree C declines from \$92.6 billion to \$85.7 billion (a 7.4\% reduction), and under 2\textdegree C from \$56.0 billion to \$54.5 billion (2.7\%). The DMO floor is more effective under severe scenarios because the domestic price cap binds only when international prices fall below \$70/t, which occurs earlier and more persistently under 1.5\textdegree C. These results confirm that the DMO provides a partial but meaningful buffer against stranding, particularly for firms with high domestic sales shares.

\subsubsection{Alternative Default Classification}

Our baseline classifies firms as defaulting when $NPV^s < 0$, which may overstate default risk for firms with substantial cash reserves. We test a stricter criterion requiring both $NPV^s < 0$ \textit{and} a cash-to-debt ratio below 0.5. Under this dual criterion, the number of defaulting firms under 1.5\textdegree C falls from 9 to 2, as several firms with negative NPV maintain sufficient liquidity to service debt over the medium term. This finding highlights the distinction between long-run economic unviability and near-term default: while 9 firms are structurally unprofitable under 1.5\textdegree C, only 2 lack the cash buffers to avoid near-term distress.

\subsubsection{Network Concentration}

To assess the role of borrower concentration in driving system-level losses, we conduct a targeted removal exercise: sequentially excluding the top 1, 2, 3, and 5 coal companies (ranked by total bank exposure) from the stress test and observing the reduction in aggregate direct credit losses. Removing the single largest borrower (DSSA, with \$862 million in bank exposure) reduces 1.5\textdegree C direct losses by 16.1\%. Removing the top 5 borrowers reduces losses by 73.6\%, confirming that the bank-coal network is dominated by a small number of high-exposure nodes. This concentration creates both risk and opportunity: targeted transition support for the top 5 coal borrowers would substantially reduce the banking sector's aggregate climate exposure.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}

Our analysis is subject to several limitations that should be borne in mind when interpreting the results.

\textit{Static analysis.} Our framework is cross-sectional, computing stranded values, credit losses, and macroeconomic impacts at a point in time rather than modelling the dynamic transition path. In reality, coal stranding would unfold over years or decades, allowing firms and banks to adjust their strategies, and the macroeconomic effects would depend on the speed and predictability of the transition. Our estimates should be interpreted as the cumulative present-value impact under a given scenario, not as a single-year GDP shock.

\textit{Incomplete exposure data.} While our bipartite network captures the largest bank-coal credit relationships, 46.2\% of total coal sector lending is classified under aggregate categories (foreign banks, syndicated loans) that cannot be traced to individual institutions. Our stress test therefore underestimates total system exposure and may miss material risks in banks not included in our sample.

\textit{No second-round effects.} Our credit loss model does not incorporate second-round amplification mechanisms such as fire sales of coal assets, contagion through the interbank market, or feedback from sovereign stress to bank balance sheets. \citet{Roncoroni2021} demonstrate that such second-round effects can double or triple first-round losses in concentrated financial networks. Our estimates should therefore be viewed as a lower bound on potential banking system losses.

\textit{Partial coverage of coal value chain.} Our analysis focuses on coal mining companies and does not explicitly model stranding risk in coal-fired power generation, coal logistics (ports, railways), or coal service providers. Banks with significant exposure to these adjacent sectors face additional transition risk not captured in our framework.

\textit{Scenario uncertainty.} The probability weights ($\pi_{BAU} = 0.30$, $\pi_{2C} = 0.40$, $\pi_{1.5C} = 0.30$) are inherently subjective, though the tornado analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:tornado}) shows that scenario weights produce the smallest variation in aggregate stranded value among all parameters tested.

\textit{Static versus dynamic transition path.} Our framework compares present values under alternative steady states rather than modelling dynamic transition trajectories. A rapid, disorderly transition would generate more acute near-term stress than a gradual, well-signalled one. Incorporating dynamic pathways would require an agent-based or DSGE framework; preliminary evidence from European stress tests \citep{ECB2022} suggests that transition speed is among the most important determinants of financial impact.
